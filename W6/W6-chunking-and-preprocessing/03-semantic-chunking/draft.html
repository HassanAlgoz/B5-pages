“Garbage In, Hallucination Out”

  • Information Asymmetry: LLMs hallucinate to bridge gaps we created.
  • The Hallucination Chain:
    1. Query: “Is 160 degrees safe?”
    2. Retrieval: Only gets the “150 degrees” fragment.
    3. The Gap: LLM doesn’t see the “Shutdown” rule.

Result: A high-risk system failure due to a character-count constraint.

Embeddings: The Semantic Sensor

How do we calculate “distance” without reading every word?

  1. Input: Feed two sentences into an Embedding Model.
  2. Process: Model converts “vibe” into a Vector (list of numbers).
  3. Calculation: Use Cosine Similarity to measure the overlap.
  • Demystify embeddings: they are not black boxes, they are “Semantic Thermometers.”
  • Explain Cosine Similarity as a simple geometric overlap check.
  • High overlap (90%) = Stay in chunk. Low overlap (40%) = Break chunk.

The Architect’s Blueprint: Algorithm Logic

  • The Goal: Move from “Mental Model” to a reproducible pipeline.
  • The Strategy: Treat text as a Signal Processing problem.
  • The Process: A 4-step modular workflow to identify semantic “breaks.”

  • We are moving from the “Rollercoaster” analogy to actual engineering.
  • Explain that treatnig text as a signal allows us to use mathematical thresholds rather than “guessing” chunk sizes.

Step 1: Sentence Tokenization

Defining the “Atomic Unit”

  • The Atom: The Sentence, not the character or word.
  • The Logic: Sentences represent the smallest unit of a complete thought.
  • Avoid “Dirty” Data:
    • Use robust libraries (NLTK, SpaCy).
    • Handle edge cases: “Dr. Smith” or “e.g.” shouldn’t trigger a split.

Input

“The revenue grew by 10%. Dr. Smith reported this on Tuesday.”

Output

  1. “The revenue grew by 10%.”
  2. “Dr. Smith reported this on Tuesday.”
  • Why not fixed-length? Because fixed-length cuts thoughts in half.
  • Mention that the quality of the tokenizer dictates the quality of the entire downstream RAG pipeline.

Step 2: Generating Embeddings

Translating Meaning to Math

  • Vectorization: Convert each sentence \(s_i\) into a high-dimensional vector \(v_i\).
  • Model Choice: Use efficient Transformers (e.g., all-MiniLM-L6-v2).
  • The “Context Window” Enhancement:
    • Don’t just compare \(s_1\) to \(s_2\).
    • Compare Sliding Windows (average of sentences \([1,2]\) vs \([3,4]\)).
    • Benefit: Smooths out noise from short transitional phrases like “However,”.
  • Explain that a single word like “However” has a very different embedding than a full sentence, which can cause “false drops” in similarity.
  • Sliding windows provide “semantic momentum.”

Step 3: Cosine Similarity

Measuring the Distance in “Thought-Space”

  • The Metric: Cosine Similarity (Score of 0.0 to 1.0).
  • The Calculation: Compare adjacent pairs \((v_i, v_{i+1})\).

The Logic

  • 1.0: Semantically identical.
  • 0.0: Completely unrelated.

The Signal

  • Remind them of the “Rollercoaster” from the previous section.
  • High similarity = the “peaks” (staying on topic).
  • Low similarity = the “valleys” (topic shifts).

Step 4: The ‘Breakpoint’

Identifying the Threshold (\(T\))

How do we decide when to “cut”?

  1. Static Threshold: Hard limit (e.g., \(T = 0.6\)). Simple, but rigid.
  2. Percentile-Based: Adaptive. Set \(T\) at the bottom 5th or 10th percentile of the document’s scores.
  3. Gradient-Based: Look for the “steepest drop” in the signal.

The Final Assembly: - If \(Score > T\): Keep building the current chunk. - If \(Score \leq T\): Close chunk; start a new one.

  • Percentile-based is usually best for production because it adapts to different writing styles (legal vs. conversational).
  • Gradient-based is more complex but handles documents with very subtle transitions well.

The Architectural Flowchart

  • Emphasize the modularity: You can swap the Embedding Model (Step D) or the Threshold Logic (Step G) without breaking the rest of the pipe.
  • This is “The Architect’s” way: building interchangeable components.

Why this works for Production

  • Solves Context Fragmentation: No more cutting a “Revenue” paragraph in half.
  • Higher Retrieval Accuracy: The LLM receives a “complete thought” as context.
  • Coherent Responses: Reduced “hallucinations” caused by missing context.

Architect’s Rule: The system only cuts when the “Signal” drops—meaning the author has actually moved on.

  • Compare this to “Naive Chunking” (fixed 500 characters).
  • In Naive chunking, the “Revenue” data might be split across two chunks, making it harder for the RAG system to find the whole answer.

The Trap of Static Thresholds

The Problem

  • Hard Thresholds: e.g., “Split if distance > 0.4”
  • Legal Contracts: Dense, cohesive (Low variance).
  • Creative Stories: Erratic, shifting (High variance).
  • Result: Zero splits or “Micro-chunks.”

The Visual

  • Explain “Semantic Gravity.”
  • A hard threshold is a “one-size-fits-all” solution that fits no one.
  • Mention that technical manuals vs. conversational transcripts have different “rhythms.”

The Solution: Percentile-Based Splitting

  • Logic: Split at the \(X^{th}\) percentile of internal distances.
  • Adaptive: The threshold moves based on the document’s own “gravity.”
  • Focus: Capturing the top 5% or 2% of “Meaning Shifts.”

  • Explain that we look at the document as a whole before cutting.
  • Use the 95th percentile as a starting point.
  • Tip: Use 90th for technical docs (subtle shifts) and 98th for news (broad shifts).

Handling Noise: The Buffer Window

  • The Danger: “Click here” or “Page 42” creates false peaks.
  • The Safeguard: Use a Moving Average.
  • Compare the average of the last 3 sentences to the next 3.
  • Smoothing: Flattens the “Semantic Rollercoaster.”
  • Explain that single-sentence comparisons are too “jittery.”
  • Analogy: Like a shock absorber on a car—it ignores the small pebbles to focus on the big bumps.
